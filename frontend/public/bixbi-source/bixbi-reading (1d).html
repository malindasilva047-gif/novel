Chapter 67

The Final Ember

Updated 2 days ago·~2,800 words·12 min read

The sun had not risen in three days. Kael stood at the edge of the broken Solarium, his boots grinding against shattered glass that had once captured the light of a thousand mornings, and he looked at her. She was not the girl he had found half-buried in the river two seasons past. She was not the frightened thing who had woken screaming in the dark with her palms on fire.

She was Solenne. Daughter of the Radiant God. And right now, she was the only thing standing between the dying world and absolute night.

"You don't have to do this," he said. His voice came out quieter than he intended. The wind pulled at it, scattered it across the ruins like ash.

"Yes, I do." She didn't look at him. Her eyes were fixed on the place where the horizon should have been — where darkness had swallowed the sky whole, where the Old Veil pressed against the world like a hand against glass. "You know I do."

He crossed the distance between them in three steps. His fingers caught her wrist — not to stop her, not to beg her. Just to hold her. To have something real to hold onto while the world unmade itself around them.

"Tell me there's another way." It wasn't a demand. It was barely a question. It was the sound of a man who already knew the answer refusing to accept it.

✦ ✦ ✦

She turned to face him then, and he almost wished she hadn't. Her eyes had changed in the last hour. The amber he knew — warm, cautious, a little wary of everything including herself — had given way to something older. Something that had no business living behind human eyes.

Gold. Burning gold, the same as the light that had not come at dawn.

"There was never another way, Kael. There was only the way I chose not to see."

He thought of the night she had laughed so hard at his terrible attempt at camp cooking that she'd knocked the pot clean off the fire. He thought of the morning he'd woken to find her sitting outside their tent, speaking in soft tones to a crow who seemed, impossibly, to be listening. He thought of the first time she had reached for his hand first. Not the second time. The first.

It felt, suddenly, like every moment had been leading here — not to this ruin, not to this impossible choice, but to this: him holding her wrist in the dark while she prepared to burn herself out like a star.

"I'm not afraid," she said.

"I know." He lifted her hand to his mouth and pressed his lips against her knuckles. "I am."

Something moved across her expression. He catalogued it the way he'd catalogued every expression in every dangerous place they'd survived together: grief, recognition, love — yes, love, he could see it now, clearly, the way you can only see things when it's almost too late — and beneath all of it, that ancient golden resolve.

"When it's done," she said quietly, "the light will come back. The crops will grow. The children who can't sleep will sleep."

"And what about me?" The words left him before he could call them back. "What do I do with the fact that there's no light worth having if it isn't—"

She kissed him.

Not gently. Not like a farewell, though he supposed that's what it was. She kissed him like she was furious and grateful and terrified all at once, like she was trying to press every unsaid thing into the space of a single breath, and he held her face in his hands and kissed her back the same way, because what else was there to do?

✦ ✦ ✦

When she pulled back, her cheeks were wet. So were his. He hadn't noticed. She raised one palm toward the smothered sky, and the first crack of gold appeared — faint, thin as a thread, like a seam torn in a dark curtain.

"Stay back," she whispered. Not to him. To the dark.

The light poured out of her like something that had been waiting a very long time to be released. It was blinding and terrible and the most beautiful thing Kael had ever seen. He shielded his eyes with one arm but did not turn away.

He would not turn away from this. He would not turn away from her.

The darkness screamed — an ancient, shapeless sound, like the groan of stone giving way underground — and then the light split the sky open from horizon to horizon, and the sun returned, and Solenne—

Solenne fell.

He caught her before she reached the ground.

She was still breathing. Barely. Her pulse under his fingers was thin as a candle flame in wind, but it was there. He gathered her against his chest, felt the slow rise and fall of her lungs, tilted his face up into the sun she had brought back, and closed his eyes.

"Stubborn," he said quietly. His voice cracked on the word. He didn't bother to hide it. "Absolutely insufferable."

Against his chest, barely a whisper: "You're welcome."

He laughed. It came out broken and wet and entirely uncontrolled, the laugh of someone who has been frightened down to the bone and found themselves, impossibly, still standing. Still holding something precious. Still here.

The dawn light fell across the ruins of the Solarium. Somewhere in the city below, a bell began to ring.
